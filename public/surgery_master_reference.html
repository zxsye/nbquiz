Overarching Principles of Surgery — Full Reference

25% of exam · Minimal dedicated lecture coverage · Must build via handbook self-study, tutorials, and podcasts

Pre-operative Assessment & Risk

  • Identification of the high-risk surgical patient
  • Classification of perioperative risk using ASA score (American Anaesthesiologist Score)
  • Approaches to pre-operative investigations
  • Diabetes management in the perioperative setting
  • Antithrombotic management in the perioperative setting
  • Management of patients with chronic pain pre-operatively (including patients prescribed opioids)

Preparation for Surgery

  • Perioperative fasting (including ERAS protocols and Sip-til-send)
  • Venous thromboembolism (VTE) prophylaxis
  • Surgical antimicrobial prophylaxis
  • Preparation for theatre: informed consent, emergency bookings

Intraoperative Aspects

  • Surgical safety checklist and communication in theatre
  • Aseptic techniques and sterility
  • Local anaesthetic doses and administration
  • Surgical approaches and techniques for wound closure

Post-operative Management

  • Pain management — acute and chronic
  • IV fluid management — resuscitation, replacement, maintenance
  • Common perioperative complications: HAP, MI, delirium, urinary retention, ileus, DVT/PE
  • Fever in post-operative patients
  • Post-operative anaemia, bleeding and massive transfusions
  • Perioperative nutrition
  • Rehabilitation, mobility and function after surgery
  • Role of other specialties/models of care: acute surgical teams, orthogeriatrics/surgical geriatrics, anaesthesia, acute and chronic pain teams, intensive care, rehabilitation medicine, allied health

On the Wards — Perioperative Competencies (all 7)

  • Charting IV fluids for patients with surgical conditions
  • Management of postoperative pain: appropriate escalation of analgesics; modalities (oral, PCA, IV infusions, regional blocks); recognising common side-effects; safe prescribing practices
  • Patient with a postoperative fever: considering sources of infection; investigations; antimicrobial choices
  • Patient with postoperative anaemia: recognising active bleeding vs operative losses; when to initiate blood transfusion
  • Assess a surgical wound: recognising impaired healing (infection, haematoma, tension/dehiscence, ischaemia); choose appropriate dressings
  • Handover of a patient before and after surgery: initiating appropriate prophylactic treatments; charting clinical history and medications; handover to anaesthetics/ICU/recovery/afterhours team
  • Preparing a patient for discharge: rehabilitation requirements; discharge medications and analgesia instructions; wound care plans; surgical follow-up; GP liaison

Recommended Effort Allocation

Based on exam blueprint weightings, lecture coverage gaps, and handbook breadth per category

Overarching Principles

28–30%

25% exam weight + almost no dedicated lecture coverage = highest effort-per-mark return. The 7 tutorials are your primary resource. Must self-study: IV fluids, periop complications, pain management, VTE prophylaxis, consent, surgical safety. All 5 podcasts count here.

Acute Surgical Conditions

20–22%

20% exam weight. Acute conditions are flagged ⚡ across every handbook specialty — this is the single most cross-cutting category. Lectures cover Trauma Management, RIF Pain, Bowel Obstruction, Compartment Syndrome. Handbook-only: acute appendicitis, acute cholecystitis/cholangitis, peptic ulcer perforation, mesenteric ischaemia, testicular torsion, renal colic, SAH, acute limb ischaemia, airway obstruction.

GI Surgery (Lower + Upper)

15–18%

15% exam weight. Lower GI has 4 lectures. Upper GI has only 1 lecture (Biliary Obstruction) for a very broad handbook section — pancreatitis, oesophago-gastric cancer, peptic ulcers, hernias, and liver/biliary disease are all handbook-only. Prioritise pancreatitis, cholecystitis, and hernias for self-study.

Vascular & Cardiothoracic

10–12%

10% exam weight. AAA, PAD, Venous Disease all have lectures. Cardiothoracic (CABG, valvular, pneumothorax, lung cancer) and acute limb ischaemia have no lectures — purely handbook. Carotid stenosis and diabetes-related foot disease also lecture-absent.

Urology & Transplant

10–12%

10% exam weight. Only Prostate Cancer (20 min) and Transplantation (14 min) have lectures. Renal colic, urinary obstruction/hydronephrosis, testicular torsion, BPH, bladder cancer, renal cancer — all handbook-only. Two podcasts (urinary retention) count here.

Orthopaedics & Trauma

5–7%

5% exam weight, yet 24% of lecture time — these are already well-served by lectures. Triage revision: focus on NOF fracture (no lecture, high-yield acutely), compartment syndrome (exam-ready via lecture), bone/joint infections (no lecture). Deprioritise the shoulder trilogy during revision week.

ENT, Head & Neck, Breast

5%

5% exam weight. Breast Surgery and Thyroid Surgery have lectures. ENT topics (otitis media, hearing loss, sinus, tonsils, larynx) are lecture-absent — skim the handbook for key points but don't over-invest. Know: airway obstruction management, neck mass differential, breast cancer triple-testing and MDT, thyroid surgery complications (RLN, hypocalcaemia).

Plastics, Burns & Hand

5%

5% exam weight. Melanoma and Skin Grafting have lectures. SCC/BCC, burns management, benign lumps, soft tissue tumours, and hand surgery are lecture-absent. Know the excision margins for melanoma, wound closure techniques, and burn assessment (TBSA, depth, fluid resuscitation rule). Skin Lesion Excision (Wk2, 10 min) is easily overlooked but directly examinable.

Neuro & Ophthalmology

5%

5% exam weight. Brain Tumour lecture covers GBM/glioma/meningioma basics. However SAH, subdural haematoma, raised ICP, and CT brain interpretation are handbook-only and are very clinically important for the ward. Note: Ophthalmology has no content in this curriculum — it is named in the exam category but there are zero handbook topics or lectures for it.
