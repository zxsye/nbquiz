* **Orthopaedic Trauma**
    ** Neck of femur (NOF) fracture
        * Pathophysiology: disruption of femoral neck architecture; high-energy mechanism in young adults; low-impact falls in osteoporotic elderly
        * Surgical anatomy: medial circumflex femoral artery blood supply; intracapsular fractures — high risk of avascular necrosis (AVN); extracapsular fractures — preserved blood supply
        * Clinical presentation: shortened, externally rotated, abducted lower limb; inability to weight-bear; groin or referred knee pain
        * Investigations: AP pelvis and lateral hip plain radiographs; MRI or CT pelvis for occult fractures with high clinical suspicion
        * Management: prompt analgesia (fascia iliaca block); fluid resuscitation; early surgical fixation <48 hours; orthogeriatric co-management
        * Surgical indications: intracapsular displaced — hemiarthroplasty or total hip replacement (THR); intracapsular undisplaced — cannulated screws; extracapsular — dynamic hip screw (DHS) or intramedullary nail
        * Complications: AVN, non-union, deep vein thrombosis (DVT), pulmonary embolism (PE), delirium, pressure ulcers, high 1-year mortality
    ** Acute extremity fractures (including proximal humerus)
        * Pathophysiology: mechanical overload exceeding bone tensile strength; direct trauma, indirect torsional forces, or pathological weakening
        * Classification: Neer classification for proximal humerus (parts 1-4 based on displacement >1cm or angulation >45 degrees) — dictates conservative vs. operative approach
        * Clinical presentation: localized pain, deformity, swelling, crepitus; functional impairment; potential skin compromise
        * Investigations: plain radiographs (rule of twos — two views, two joints, two occasions); CT for complex articular involvement
        * Management: ATLS principles; immediate fracture reduction and splinting; analgesia; elevation for edema minimization
        * Surgical indications: open fractures, neurovascular compromise, intra-articular displacement, poly-trauma, failure of conservative alignment
        * Complications: malunion, delayed union, secondary osteoarthritis, adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder following proximal humerus fracture)
    ** Ankle fractures
        * Pathophysiology: rotational forces applied to the ankle mortise; malleolar avulsion or talar impact fractures
        * Classification: Danis-Weber classification: Type A (infra-syndesmotic), Type B (trans-syndesmotic), Type C (supra-syndesmotic) — determines syndesmosis integrity and surgical planning
        * Clinical presentation: localized malleolar pain, inability to weight bear (Ottawa Ankle Rules), visible deformity in fracture-dislocations
        * Investigations: weight-bearing plain radiographs (AP, lateral, mortise views); CT for pilon fractures or preoperative planning
        * Management: immediate reduction of dislocated joint; backslab cast application; non-weight bearing status
        * Surgical indications: Weber C fractures, bimalleolar/trimalleolar fractures, unstable Weber B (talar shift), open fractures
        * Complications: post-traumatic osteoarthritis, wound breakdown, complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)
    ** Paediatric fractures
        * Pathophysiology: unique biomechanics of immature bone; presence of physis (growth plate); highly vascularized thick periosteum enabling rapid remodeling
        * Classification: Salter-Harris classification (Types I-V) — defines growth plate involvement and growth arrest risk
        * Clinical presentation: localized tenderness, refusal to use limb, deformity; toddler's fracture — subtle spiral tibial fracture
        * Investigations: plain radiographs (compare with contralateral limb for ossification centers — CRITOE sequence in elbow)
        * Management: cast immobilization; manipulation under anesthesia (MUA) and K-wire fixation for unstable patterns (supracondylar humerus)
        * Surgical anatomy: Gartland classification for supracondylar fractures; proximity of anterior interosseous nerve (AIN) and brachial artery
        * Red flags: non-accidental injury (NAI) patterns — posterior rib fractures, metaphyseal corner fractures, varying ages of healing

* **Orthopaedic Emergencies & Complications**
    ** Compartment syndrome
        * Pathophysiology: elevated osteofascial intracompartmental pressure >30 mmHg; downstream consequences — capillary perfusion failure, cellular hypoxia, muscle necrosis, rhabdomyolysis
        * Clinical presentation: pain out of proportion to injury, pain on passive stretch (earliest sign), tense compartments; late signs — pallor, pulselessness, paralysis
        * Investigations: clinical diagnosis primarily; direct intracompartmental pressure monitoring (Delta pressure <30 mmHg threshold for intervention)
        * Management: removal of all constrictive dressings (split casts to skin); limb positioning at heart level (avoid elevation); urgent orthopaedic referral
        * Surgical indications: immediate four-compartment lower leg fasciotomy; delayed primary closure or skin grafting at 48-72 hours
        * Complications: Volkmann's ischaemic contracture, permanent nerve damage, acute kidney injury (AKI) secondary to myoglobinuria, limb amputation
    ** Open fractures
        * Pathophysiology: osseous disruption communicating with external environment; high risk of environmental contamination and soft tissue devascularization
        * Classification: Gustilo-Anderson classification (Grades I, II, IIIA, IIIB, IIIC) — guides antibiotic choice, soft tissue coverage needs, and amputation risk
        * Clinical presentation: visible bone, bleeding wound overlying fracture site, severe soft tissue crushing or degloving
        * Investigations: plain radiographs (assess fracture pattern and gas in soft tissues); CT angiography for suspected arterial injury (Gustilo IIIC)
        * Management: wound photography (avoid repeated examination); gross debris removal; betadine-soaked dressing; tetanus prophylaxis; nil-by-mouth (NBM) protocol
        * Pharmacotherapy: urgent IV antibiotics within 1 hour (cephazolin; add gentamicin for grade III; add penicillin for agricultural/marine contamination)
        * Surgical indications: urgent surgical debridement and skeletal stabilization (<6 hours for highly contaminated; <24 hours for others)
    ** Bone and joint infections
        * Pathophysiology: haematogenous seeding (S. aureus most common), direct inoculation, or contiguous spread to synovium or bone marrow; cartilage destruction mediated by bacterial toxins and host inflammatory response
        * Clinical presentation: monoarticular severely painful, hot, swollen joint; marked restriction of active and passive movement; fever and systemic toxicity
        * Investigations: urgent joint aspiration before antibiotics (cell count >50,000 WCC/mm3, Gram stain, culture, crystals); blood cultures; serial CRP/ESR trending; plain radiographs (baseline); MRI (gold standard for osteomyelitis)
        * Management: empirical IV antibiotics (flucloxacillin or vancomycin) adjusting to susceptibilities; transition to oral agents for prolonged 4-6 week course
        * Surgical indications: urgent arthroscopic or open joint washout and debridement for septic arthritis; surgical debridement of necrotic bone for chronic osteomyelitis
        * Complications: irreversible articular cartilage destruction (within 24 hours), growth arrest in children, chronic osteomyelitis, sepsis, joint subluxation

* **Arthroplasty & Degenerative Joint Disease**
    ** Osteoarthritis (Hip, Knee, Shoulder)
        * Pathophysiology: progressive loss of articular cartilage, subchondral bone sclerosis, osteophyte formation, secondary synovial inflammation
        * Clinical presentation: insidious onset mechanical pain; worse with weight-bearing or activity; brief morning stiffness (<30 minutes); functional decline; joint effusion and crepitus
        * Investigations: weight-bearing plain radiographs; classic radiographic features — Loss of joint space, Osteophytes, Subchondral sclerosis, Subchondral cysts (LOSS)
        * Conservative management: weight loss, structured physiotherapy, walking aids; oral analgesia (paracetamol, NSAIDs); intra-articular corticosteroid injections
        * Surgical indications: refractory pain impacting activities of daily living (ADLs) and night sleep; failed conservative management; significant deformity
        * Surgical options: total hip replacement (THR), total knee replacement (TKR), anatomic or reverse total shoulder replacement (TSA/rTSA) based on rotator cuff integrity
        * Medicolegal considerations: thorough consent detailing risks of infection, bleeding, nerve injury, leg length discrepancy (THR), stiffness (TKR), and need for future revision
    ** Post-operative care and recovery after joint replacement
        * Pathophysiology: major surgical trauma inducing hypercoagulable state and systemic inflammatory response; physiological stress requiring robust recovery protocols
        * Clinical assessment: daily neurovascular observations; wound inspection for erythema or discharge; monitoring of urine output and drain output (if present)
        * Management: enhanced recovery after surgery (ERAS) pathways; early mobilization (day 0 or 1); targeted multimodal analgesia (avoiding heavy opioids)
        * VTE prophylaxis: chemical agents (LMWH, rivaroxaban, or aspirin based on risk) combined with mechanical compression (TEDS, calf compressors) for 14-35 days
        * Medical co-management: orthogeriatric involvement for elderly patients; optimization of comorbidities (diabetes, anemia, osteoporosis)
        * Complications: periprosthetic joint infection (early vs. late presentation), aseptic loosening, dislocation (THR — adherence to hip precautions), periprosthetic fracture
        * Discharge criteria: independent mobility with frame/sticks, safe stair climbing, adequate pain control, clean dry wound

* **Hand & Upper Limb Surgery**
    ** Carpal tunnel syndrome
        * Pathophysiology: compression neuropathy of the median nerve beneath the transverse carpal ligament; exacerbated by tenosynovitis, pregnancy, trauma, or hypothyroidism
        * Clinical presentation: pain and paraesthesia in thumb, index, middle, and radial half of ring finger; nocturnal worsening (waking patient); clumsiness and dropping objects
        * Clinical examination: positive Tinel’s sign over flexor retinaculum; positive Phalen’s test; late signs — thenar eminence atrophy, fixed sensory loss
        * Investigations: clinical diagnosis usually sufficient; nerve conduction studies (NCS) and electromyography (EMG) — confirms diagnosis and quantifies demyelination/axonal loss
        * Conservative management: nocturnal volar extension splinting; localized ultrasound-guided corticosteroid injection; activity modification
        * Surgical indications: refractory symptoms, motor weakness (thenar atrophy), severe axonal loss on NCS
        * Surgical anatomy: release of transverse carpal ligament; anatomical risk to recurrent motor branch of median nerve ("million dollar nerve")
    ** Hand and tendon injuries
        * Pathophysiology: disruption of flexor or extensor mechanisms, neurovascular bundles, or phalangeal architecture via laceration, crush, or avulsion
        * Clinical presentation: specific resting hand posture abnormalities (loss of normal flexor cascade); localized lacerations; digital nerve sensory deficits (two-point discrimination >5mm)
        * Clinical examination: isolated testing of Flexor Digitorum Profundus (FDP — DIPJ flexion) and Flexor Digitorum Superficialis (FDS — PIPJ flexion); Allen’s test for digital artery perfusion
        * Investigations: plain radiographs of hand/digits (exclude foreign bodies, fractures, joint penetration)
        * Management: washout and temporary sterile dressing; elevation; tetanus prophylaxis; empiric oral antibiotics for contaminated wounds/bites
        * Surgical indications: tendon lacerations require formal exploration and primary repair (within 7-10 days); digital nerve or vessel repair under microscopy
        * Complications: tendon adhesions, rupture of primary repair, joint stiffness, chronic regional pain syndrome (CRPS), missed foreign body
    ** Shoulder dislocation and instability
        * Pathophysiology: structural failure of the glenohumeral stabilizers (labrum, capsule, ligaments); anterior dislocation accounts for >95%, typically from forced abduction and external rotation
        * Clinical presentation: squared-off shoulder contour; prominent acromion; arm held in slight abduction and external rotation; severe pain and resistance to movement
        * Clinical examination: axillary nerve function assessment (sensation over regimental badge area, deltoid contraction) pre- and post-reduction
        * Investigations: AP, scapular Y, and axillary plain radiographs; identify associated Bankart (glenoid lip) or Hill-Sachs (humeral head) lesions
        * Management: prompt closed reduction using traction-countertraction, Cunningham, or Kocher techniques; adequate procedural sedation and analgesia
        * Post-reduction care: shoulder immobilizer sling (internal rotation) for 2-4 weeks; targeted physiotherapy emphasizing dynamic rotator cuff stabilization
        * Complications: recurrent instability (inversely correlated with age of first dislocation), axillary nerve neuropraxia, rotator cuff tear (especially in patients >40 years)

* **Basic Surgical Principles & Skills**
    ** Wound healing and closure
        * Pathophysiology: sequential phases — haemostasis (platelet plug), inflammation (neutrophils/macrophages), proliferation (fibroblasts, angiogenesis), remodelling (collagen cross-linking)
        * Clinical assessment: wound classification — clean, clean-contaminated, contaminated, dirty/infected; influences closure strategy and infection risk
        * Closure options: primary intention (sutures, staples, glue); secondary intention (granulation and contraction for contaminated wounds); delayed primary closure
        * Surgical techniques: correct approximation of tissue layers; elimination of dead space; eversion of skin edges; appropriate suture selection (absorbable for deep layers, non-absorbable/staples for skin)
        * Local anaesthesia: lidocaine (max 3mg/kg, or 7mg/kg with adrenaline) vs. bupivacaine (max 2mg/kg); adrenaline contraindications — digits, nose, penis, pinna
        * Complications: surgical site infection (SSI), wound dehiscence, hypertrophic scarring, keloid formation, haematoma/seroma
        * Red flags: spreading erythema, purulent discharge, systemic sepsis, crepitus (necrotizing fasciitis)
    ** Surgical fluid management
        * Pathophysiology: physiological shifts of body water across fluid compartments during surgical stress; third-spacing in major abdominal/trauma surgery; obligate insensible losses
        * Clinical presentation of hypovolaemia: tachycardia, hypotension, prolonged capillary refill, oliguria (<0.5 mL/kg/hr), altered sensorium
        * Investigations: urea and electrolytes (U&E), creatinine, fluid balance chart, serum lactate (tissue perfusion marker), central venous oxygen saturation (ScvO2)
        * Resuscitation principles: 250-500 mL crystalloid fluid bolus (Plasmalyte or Hartmann's); reassess response (HR, BP, urine output); avoid 0.9% saline in large volumes due to hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis
        * Maintenance requirements: 25-30 mL/kg/day water; 1 mmol/kg/day sodium, potassium, chloride; 50-100 g/day glucose to prevent starvation ketosis
        * Replacement considerations: matching specific losses (gastric aspirate — 0.9% saline with KCl; biliary/pancreatic — Hartmann's); accounting for intraoperative bleeding and insensible losses
        * Complications: fluid overload (pulmonary oedema, peripheral oedema, bowel wall oedema); inadequate resuscitation consequences — AKI and multi-organ failure
