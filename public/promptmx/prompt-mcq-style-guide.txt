# Medical MCQ Style Guide
*Single-best-answer questions for final-year medical students*

---

## CORE PRINCIPLE
Every question tests the **next best step**, not merely a valid step. Distractors must be plausible and from the same category as the correct answer — students should need to reason through *why* each distractor fails, not just recognise it as wrong.

**Before generating each question, reason through:**
1. What concept am I testing?
2. What would a student plausibly choose instead, and why?
3. Does the stem contain exactly one discriminating detail that separates the correct answer from all distractors?

Then write the question.

---

## ANSWER OPTIONS
- Always **5 options (A–E)**, one single best answer.
- Distractors must be: the right action at the wrong time; the right treatment for a related but distinct condition; a valid step that isn't the *most appropriate* given the specific context; or the next step in the sequence rather than the current one.

---

## QUESTION FORMATS

### 1. Clinical Vignette (majority of questions)
2–5 sentences: patient demographics, chief complaint, relevant history, examination findings, pertinent results. Occasionally include irrelevant history to train filtering.

- **Diagnosis** — enough discriminating features to favour one diagnosis; ask *"most likely diagnosis"* or *"most important to exclude"*
- **Investigation** — specify what's already been done; distractors are too invasive, less specific, or premature
- **Acute management** — time-critical scenario; most common trap is treating before stabilising or skipping a diagnostic step
- **Definitive / next step** — initial action already described; tests sequence of care, not isolated steps; highest-yield format
- **Complication recognition** — post-procedure patient with temporal clues (e.g., "day 2 post-op"); ask for cause or next step
- **Pathophysiology / mechanism** — underlying mechanism, expected investigation finding, or drug rationale

### 2. Direct Fact-Based
1–2 sentence stem for foundational knowledge not suited to a vignette. Epidemiology, classification, pharmacology. Distractors must span a clinically meaningful range — not trivially obvious.

### 3. Sequential / Multi-Step Management
State the scenario → state the initial action taken → describe patient response → ask for next best step. Each distractor is a step that is premature, already done, or appropriate for a different clinical trajectory.

### 4. Lab / Imaging Interpretation
Brief vignette + lab values (electrolytes, LFTs, ABG, coagulation) or imaging description. Ask for diagnosis or next action. Distractors have overlapping biochemical or radiological profiles.

### 5. Exception Stem ("Which is NOT…")
Four options are genuine associations; the correct answer is plausible but belongs to a related, distinct condition. Tests incomplete mental models. Never use when the exception is trivially obvious.

### 6. Applied Anatomy / Procedure
Frame around a clinical consequence. Distractors are nearby anatomical structures plausible in the procedural context.

## BLOOM'S LEVEL MIX
| Level      | Target | Examples                                                         |
| ---------- | ------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Remember   | 15%    | Fact-based, classification                                       |
| Understand | 25%    | Pathophysiology, lab interpretation                              |
| Apply      | 35%    | Vignette → diagnosis or initial management                       |
| Analyse    | 25%    | Sequential management, complication recognition, exception stems |

---

## EACH QUESTION MUST INCLUDE AN EXPLANATION COVERING:
1. Why the correct answer is correct
2. Why the most tempting distractor is wrong
3. A clinical pearl or decision rule where applicable

---

## AVOID
- "All of the above" / "None of the above"
- Stem clues that make the answer obvious without reasoning
- Two answers that are equally defensible — add one discriminating clinical detail if needed
- Pure recall questions unless the fact is genuinely high-yield
